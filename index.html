I'm currently in my senior year at UT Austin, where I'm continuing to work on MayaLEX, NahuatLEX, and personal projects alongside my studies.

As a Mexican-American from Austin whose parents both came from Mexico, I've grown up with the advantage of being bilingual — something that has shaped my outlook, my identity, and my sense of what's worth working on. My study of linguistics gives that engineering work somewhere specific to point: problems at the intersection of language and computation that aren't well served otherwise.

As I keep building, I try to stay honest about the tools I pick and the implications of those choices.

Projects

Trucking Logistics Mobile App

January 2025 · React Native, Expo, TypeScript, Node.js, Tesseract.js, Anthropic API, Heroku, Firebase

A mobile app that helps owner/operator trucking companies manage their paperwork digitally instead of drowning in receipts. The app captures images and sends them as base64 to a Node.js backend on Heroku, which uses Tesseract.js to extract the text; the extracted text is then sent to Anthropic's API to classify and structure the receipt data — fuel purchases, mileage, expenses — before returning it to the frontend.

Smart reporting on top of that generates tax documents and financial summaries, which apparently saves truckers hours of manual work every week. CI/CD runs through GitHub Actions and EAS Build. Started as a solution for one company; now has 50+ active users across different operations.

Empire Football Group Platform

December 2024, actively maintained · Next.js, TypeScript, Cloudflare, MongoDB, PayPal

A full organization-management system for a local Austin soccer club that was still running on spreadsheets and group chats. The site manages three teams under one organization and serves as both a promotional asset and an internal management tool. Computational Linguistics Research Assistant — MayaLEX

Linguistics Research Center, UT Austin · August 2023 – Present

MayaLEX is a collection of lexical resources for early Mayan languages — a digital initiative for indigenous languages where one can browse etymological dictionaries like Colonial Ch'olti', Colonial Kaqchikel, Colonial K'iche', and more. The signature feature is linking words across associated languages. The underlying work involved processing and analyzing 50,000+ Mayan language entries drawn from colonial and modern sources. I wrote Python automation that cut Excel-dataset processing time substantially and implemented classification scripts that pull out specific linguistic features and attributes. The project is far from finished, but we shipped a robust prototype that lays the groundwork for similar projects across other Mesoamerican language families.

MayaLEX matters to me because of the intended audience and purpose. Indigeneity in the archive is a practical and ethical challenge that has to inform how applications like this get built — and that applies to the programming side as much as anywhere else.

Computational Linguistics Research Assistant — NahuatLEX

Linguistics Research Center, UT Austin · April 2025 – Present

NahuatLEX is the V2 of the MayaLEX prototype. Working on MayaLEX taught me how much version control matters when you're comparing different datasets and outputs of Python scripts, so NahuatLEX is the project where I'm putting that lesson into practice. It's a collection of lexical resources for the Nahuatl language family. The sources are quite different from MayaLEX's, so pre-processing has looked different too — much more parsing of HTML tags and cleaning up source code. The upside is that the production deployment pipeline from MayaLEX transfers; the hard part is getting the data ready to flow through it.

No public launch yet, but the repo is updated frequently.

Fifth Sun: A New History of the Aztecs — Camilla Townsend

Currently reading

In November 1519, Hernando Cortés walked along a causeway leading to the capital of the Aztec kingdom and came face to face with Moctezuma. That story has been told many times, but always from the Spanish side. The Native Americans were intrigued by the Roman alphabet and, unbeknownst to the newcomers, used it to write detailed histories in their own language of Nahuatl. Fifth Sun tells the history of the Aztecs based solely on texts written by the indigenous people themselves — a humanized depiction rather than the exotic, bloody figures of European stereotypes.
